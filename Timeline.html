A referendum on ending apartheid was held in South Africa on 17 March 1992. The referendum was limited to white South African voters, who were asked whether or not they supported the negotiated reforms begun by State President F. W. de Klerk two years earlier, in which he proposed to end the apartheid system that had been implemented since 1948. The result of the election was a large victory for the "yes" side, which ultimately resulted in apartheid being lifted. Universal suffrage was introduced two years later.

In 1994, South Africa transitioned from the National Party, which upheld apartheid since 1948, to the African National Congress (ANC), previously outlawed for opposing apartheid. The ANC won the first multiracial election with universal suffrage, breaking the previous white-exclusive voting system. Violence and conflicts arose in Bantustans before the elections, with some leaders opposing participation, while citizens sought inclusion. Incidents included bombings targeting the ANC and National Party, along with politically-motivated murders involving leaders of the ANC and Inkatha Freedom Party (IFP).

The adoption of the South African Constitution on 8 May 1996 was one of the turning points in the history of the struggle for democracy in this country. The Constitution is considered by many as one of the most advanced in the world, with a Bill of Rights second to none

In 1993, a definitive date was established to conclude white minority rule in South Africa. After the suspension of multiparty negotiations in June 1992, discussions were resumed. April 27, 1994, was determined as the pivotal date for conducting the inaugural multiracial general election in South Africa, marking a significant milestone in the nation's history.

In 1995, the interim constitution agreed to during the negotiations to end apartheid was replaced by a new constitution. The government also established the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) to expose the crimes of the apartheid era.
